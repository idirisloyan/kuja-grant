1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
================================================================================

This quarterly accountability report presents findings from CRI's Community Feedback and Response Mechanism (CFRM) operating across 24 target communities in Garissa and Wajir Counties. During Q4 2025, the CFRM received a total of 287 feedback entries (complaints, suggestions, compliments, and queries), representing a 23% increase from Q3 (233 entries). This increase is attributed to expanded awareness campaigns and the activation of two additional feedback channels (WhatsApp and community radio).

Of the 287 entries received, 241 (84%) were resolved within the 14-day standard response time, 34 (12%) required extended investigation and were resolved within 30 days, and 12 (4%) remain open and under active management. The overall beneficiary satisfaction rate, as measured through the quarterly perception survey (n=480), was 81%, an improvement from 74% in Q3 2025.

The report also presents findings from the annual beneficiary satisfaction survey conducted in November 2025 across all program locations, with a sample of 720 beneficiaries representing all demographic groups.

This report is structured in alignment with Core Humanitarian Standard (CHS) Commitment 5 (Complaints are welcomed and addressed) and Commitment 4 (Communication and participation).

3.3 ANALYSIS OF HIGH-SEVERITY COMPLAINTS

Beneficiary Selection/Targeting (18 complaints):
Twelve complaints related to perceived exclusion of eligible households from food distribution in Modogashe sub-location. Investigation revealed that the October 2025 re-targeting exercise inadvertently removed 8 households that had temporarily relocated during the verification period but remained vulnerable. Six complaints related to inclusion of perceived non-eligible individuals in livelihood programs in Wajir. Post-distribution monitoring confirmed that 3 cases involved clerical errors in registration (corrected immediately) and 3 were unfounded (beneficiaries met all eligibility criteria).

Actions taken: The 8 excluded households in Modogashe were reinstated in the November distribution cycle. A revised verification protocol now includes a 7-day community review period with public posting of beneficiary lists (anonymized codes) before finalization. Community targeting committees received refresher training on vulnerability criteria.

Staff Behavior/Attitudes (14 complaints):
Seven complaints related to a specific community mobilizer in Habaswein who was described as using disrespectful language during household visits. Four complaints related to perceived favoritism by distribution staff. Three complaints reported long wait times without explanation or apology from staff.

Actions taken: The community mobilizer in Habaswein received a formal written warning and mandatory refresher training on CRI's Code of Conduct and respectful communication. All distribution teams received training on beneficiary communication and managing expectations. A "services charter" poster was developed and displayed at all distribution points, stating beneficiary rights and expected staff conduct.

Privacy Concerns (4 complaints):
Four complaints from women in Wajir reported that registration questions about household income and assets were asked in public settings, causing embarrassment and potential protection concerns.

Actions taken: Registration protocols revised to require private interview spaces. All registration staff retrained on data protection and privacy standards. Mobile registration screens (portable privacy barriers) procured and deployed.

Resolution process:
1. All feedback is logged in a centralized database within 24 hours of receipt.
2. Each entry is categorized by type, severity, and sector.
3. Acknowledgment is provided to the complainant within 48 hours (where contact information is available).
4. Low-severity complaints are addressed by the relevant program team within 7 days.
5. Medium-severity complaints are escalated to the Program Manager for resolution within 14 days.
6. High-severity complaints are escalated to the Country Director and investigated within 14 days, with potential extension to 30 days.
7. Sensitive complaints (SEA, fraud, corruption) are immediately escalated to the Executive Director and handled through the safeguarding investigation protocol.
8. All complainants are informed of the outcome of their complaint and any actions taken.

9. LESSONS LEARNED AND RECOMMENDATIONS
================================================================================

Lessons Learned:

1. Multi-channel feedback approach increases accessibility: The introduction of WhatsApp and community radio channels in Q4 reached demographics (youth, pastoralists in remote areas) that were previously underrepresented in feedback data. The diversity of channels is essential for capturing comprehensive community perspectives.

2. Community feedback facilitators bridge literacy barriers: Trained community members who assist illiterate and elderly people in providing feedback have been critical. Communities with active facilitators generated 40% more feedback entries than those without.

3. Closing the feedback loop builds trust: Communities where CRI consistently communicated back on actions taken in response to complaints showed a 15-percentage-point increase in willingness to provide future feedback compared to the baseline.

4. Disaggregated satisfaction data reveals inequities: The lower satisfaction rates among persons with disabilities would not have been identified without deliberate disaggregation and oversampling of this group. Standard surveys risk masking the experiences of marginalized populations.

Recommendations:

1. Invest in making all distribution and service points physically accessible for persons with disabilities, including ramps, seating priority, and dedicated assistance.
2. Increase frequency of feedback analysis from quarterly to monthly to enable faster programmatic response.
3. Develop a real-time feedback dashboard accessible to all program managers for immediate awareness of emerging trends.
4. Expand community-led monitoring approach, enabling beneficiary representatives to conduct joint monitoring visits.
5. Strengthen the feedback mechanism's capacity to capture feedback from hard-to-reach pastoralist communities through mobile outreach.

================================================================================
10. CONCLUSION
================================================================================

The Q4 2025 accountability report demonstrates continued strengthening of CRI's Community Feedback and Response Mechanism. The increase in total feedback received (23% growth), improvement in resolution rates (84% within 14 days), and rising beneficiary satisfaction (81%, up from 62% at baseline) indicate that the accountability system is functioning effectively and contributing to program quality.

Key areas for continued improvement include: strengthening accessibility for persons with disabilities, increasing community participation in program governance (currently 54% of beneficiaries report having input opportunities), and reducing the 28% of beneficiaries who still do not know how to make a complaint.

CRI remains committed to the principle that accountability to affected populations is not an add-on but a core component of quality humanitarian programming, in full alignment with the Core Humanitarian Standard.
